Warp Speed Travel

©2022 Glenn Eychaner

DISCLAIMER: Nothing in this essay is “canon.” It was inspired by finding a copy of “Star Trek Technology,” ©1988 Leon Myerson, in some old filing boxes, and updates and improves upon his thoughts and equations regarding warp travel.

Ships in Star Trek travel at apparent faster-than-light speeds by using a “warp drive” to generate a “warp field” which bends space and subspace, permitting the ship to exceed the speed of light and break the conventional laws of inertia, momentum, kinetic energy, and special relativity. The speed of the ship is often given as the “warp factor.” However, the warp factor does not directly indicate the speed, but instead the energy density of the generated warp field. (Thus, a larger ship, which will require a larger warp field, will also require more warp power and a larger warp core than a smaller ship to reach the same warp factor.) Warp 1 is the field density required to travel at the speed of light in spacetime (c); Warp 2 is twice that field density, and so on. At warp factors less than Warp 1, often referred to as “impulse power,” the ship, though traveling via the warp field effect, is traveling slower than the speed of light.

The formula relating the warp factor (W) to apparent speed is not the inadequate V = W3c. The United Federation of Planets' Treaty Exploration Zone mapped in the Starfleet Technical Manual has a radius of 4,750 parsecs, or over 15,000 light years, with the Klingon and Romulan empires located beyond Neutral Zones at the boundary some 60 degrees apart. At a maximum safe cruising speed of Warp 6, or 216c, a Constitution-class cruiser would require in excess of 70 years to reach the Federation boundary from the center. Even at the speed given in Leon Myerson's document for Warp 6 (1,927c), it would take 8 years to reach the Neutral Zones. The result is referred to as an apparent speed because it does not have the same properties (momentum, inertia, or kinetic energy) as a true velocity in spacetime. In fact, a ship which shuts down its warp engines will decelerate from whatever speed it was traveling to a near halt relative to the local spacetime environment as the warp field around the ship gradually collapses. Thus, we will continue to use the term “speed” rather than “velocity” to refer to the motion of a starship under warp power.

It should be noted that the Cochrane equation Æ(W) above is a simplification that is only valid in a spacetime free of extreme gravitational, electromagnetic, and other fields and gradients, and quantum or subspace distortions. In the presence of these factors, the Cochrane equation becomes much more complicated, leading to effects such as a ship which accelerates suddenly to a high warp factor while extremely close to an object of stellar mass potentially traveling backward in time (assuming it is not destroyed in the process).

From the earliest days of starship operations, the speeds produced by warp fields were found to be smaller than predicted. At first, engineers blamed inefficiencies in the ships' reactors, warp cores, warp coils, or warp nacelles, but after further investigation, it was found that W³Æc only describes the apparent spacetime motion of a theoretical dimensionless warp field in subspace. Real warp engines and the fields they produce exist simultaneously in spacetime and subspace and have non-zero dimensions, which produces a “continuum drag” effect. When an Intrepid-class starship was trapped in the Delta Quadrant, approximately 60,000 light years from Federation space, its crew estimated that the travel time necessary to reach Federation space would be approximately 70 years. This indicates that it could not sustain a speed above Warp 3 over that distance, likely due to problems supplying fuel and energy faster than it was consumed at higher speeds. For a Federation vessel to travel at its maximum safe cruising speed of of Warp 6 over any significant distance, therefore, likely depends on the extensive network of starbases, base stations, and friendly planets established by the Federation within their space.

When Secondary warp drives were developed, the terminology of vessel design adopted the convention of referring to the hull housing the Secondary drive system as the secondary hull, and the other housing the impulse drive with its Primary drive system as the primary hull. With the advent of Tertiary drive systems, there were still only two drive systems and two main hull sections, so the one with the larger engine system continued to be called the secondary hull.

The impulse drive in the Primary hull, also known as the “saucer section,” is usually only capable of propelling the ship at sub-light speeds. However, after a ship separation, the saucer section alone requires a much smaller warp field to enclose it and thus can travel at speeds faster than light, allowing it to escape to a nearby base or planet.

The speed of such a “Nullary” drive with no energy supplied would be 0.145 times the speed of light, allowing rapid interplanetary travel at low or no energy cost. Though subspace particles are known to obey this equation (and, in fact, cannot be observed at rest in spacetime as a result), Federation scientists have been unable to take advantage of this phenomenon.

Another effect of this phenomenon is that the “bubble” of spacetime “carried along” by any warp drive containing the generating ship is subject to the relativistic effects of the “zero-energy nullary” speed of 0.145c, though that only results in a time dilation of about 1%. Due to the complex interactions at the boundary between the “bubble” of spacetime and the warp field, in any instant of time, the ship and its occupants observe the outside universe (when observing using conventional particles such as photons of light) as though they are traveling at 0.145c relative to their state before activating the warp field. However, this view is inconsistent from one instant to the next, as the apparent positions of objects will change at a rate consistent with the ship's actual apparent speed. A ship traveling at a speed faster than light will also observe objects behind it appearing to move backward in time, as the ship passes the photons emitted by the object, and will observe objects in front off it appearing to move forward in time at an accelerated rate. For this reason, standard ship's sensors can not only detect conventional particles, but also subspace particles and effects which can move at speeds up to and even beyond an effective speed of Warp 10.
